Kinetic Energy for Rigid Bodies

The concepts of Work and Energy provide the basis for solving a variety of kinetics problems. In rigid bodies, as with particles, we will break energy into kinetic energy and potential energy.

Kinetic energy is the energy of mass in motion, while potential energy represents the energy that is stored up due to the position or stresses in a body. Because rigid bodies have extent, they have two types of motion that can store kinetic energy - linear and angular motion.

The kinetic energy of a rigid body due to linear motion is one half of the mass of the body times the resultant velocity at its centre of mass squared.

The kinetic energy of a rigid body due to rotational motion is one half of the mass moment of inertia about centre of mass times the angular velocity squared. The total kinetic energy is the sum of linear and rotational kinetic energy for the body.

\[KE=\frac{1}{2}mv_G^{2}+\frac{1}{2}I_G\omega^{2}\]

There are two alternate forms of the kinetic energy equation that can be useful. If the body is pinned at a point O, then the equation simplifies to:

\[KE=\frac{1}{2}I_O\omega^{2}\]

If there is an ICZV for a rigid body in general plane motion, then the equation simplifies to:

\[KE=\frac{1}{2}I_{IC}\omega^{2}\]

Kinetic energy is a scalar value, so we will use resultant linear velocities. Also, kinetic energy is always positive. Note that there is no option to use the linear velocity at another point besides the centre of gravity.

If we wish to determine the change in kinetic energy, we would simply take the final kinetic energy minus the initial kinetic energy.

\[\Delta KE=\left (\frac{1}{2}mv_{Gf}^{2}+ \frac{1}{2}I_G\omega_{f}^{2} \right )- \left( \frac{1}{2}mv_{Gi}^{2} + \frac{1}{2}I_G\omega_{i}^{2}\right )\]
