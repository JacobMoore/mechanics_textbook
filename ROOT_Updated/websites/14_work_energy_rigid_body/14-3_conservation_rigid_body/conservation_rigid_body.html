The Conservation of Energy for a Rigid Body or a System of Rigid Bodies

Conservation of energy is what you get when there is no non-conservative work done. As a reminder, springs and gravity do conservative work, but these work values are reframed as potential energy. If there are no other external forces, such as kinetic friction or applied forces, that are doing work, then we can say that energy is conserved. Generally, this means that the same amount of energy is converted between different types. For example, a ball is held stationary 1 m above the ground at state 1. At state 2, the ball is falling and is just about to impact the ground. In this case, gravitational potential energy (mg(1m)) has been converted into linear kinetic energy (1/2mvG2).

\[KE_1 + PE_1=KE_2 + PE_2\]
or
\[\Delta KE + \Delta PE=0\]

Conservation of Energy in Systems of Rigid Body Problems:

One of the more valuable ways to use conservation of energy is to consider a connected system of rigid bodies. Recall that internal forces do not do work. If we include multiple rigid bodies in our system, then the contact forces between the bodies become internal forces, and we do not need to calculate the work done by them. (If we still have external forces, then we need to go back to the principle of work-energy equation, and include work).

\[\Sigma_{sys} KE_{1,i} + \Sigma_{sys} PE_{1,i} =\Sigma_{sys} KE_{2,i} + \Sigma_{sys} PE_{2,i}\]
or
\[\Sigma_{sys} \Delta(KE_{i}) + \Sigma_{sys} \Delta(PE_{i}) =0\]

over all rigid bodies, i = 1, 2, ..., in the system.
