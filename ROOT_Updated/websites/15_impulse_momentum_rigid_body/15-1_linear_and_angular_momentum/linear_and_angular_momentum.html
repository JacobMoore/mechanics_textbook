Linear and Angular Momentum for a Rigid Body

As discussed with particles, the linear momentum of a body will be equal to the mass of the body times its current velocity. Since velocity is a vector, the momentum will also be a vector, having both magnitude and a direction. Unlike the impulse, which happens over some set time, the momentum is captured as a snapshot of a specific instant in time (usually right before and after some impulse is exerted). The units for linear momentum will be mass times unit distance per unit time. This is usually kilogram meters per second in metric, or slug feet per second in English units.

It is important to note that, since velocity is not necessarily the same at all points on a rigid body, linear momentum specifically includes the velocity at the centre of gravity only. There is no other version of linear momentum.

Note that linear momentum, which we will denote as J, is sometimes represented by L or G symbolically.

\[\vec{J} = m \vec{v}_G\]

Angular momentum on the other hand will be equal to the mass moment of inertia of that body (about it's center of mass) times it's current angular velocity. It's important to note that while the mass moment of inertia remains constant if the body does not change shape, a shape that does change shape will likely have changes in mass moments of inertia along with changes in angular velocity.

We will denote angular momentum as K, however many sources also use H to represent angular momentum.

Just like there are several versions of the moment equation from Newton's Second Law, there are several versions of angular momentum for a rigid body, depending on the point about which you take your angular impulses. Since the angular velocity of a rigid body is the same at all locations, the difference in the angular momentum expression comes from differences in the mass moment of inertia.

If we want to find the angular momentum about the centre of gravity of a body, the equation is:

\[\vec{K}_G = I_G \vec{\omega}\]

If our object is pinned at a point O, the expression for angular momentum about O is:

\[\vec{K}_O = I_O \vec{\omega}\]

If our object has an ICZV, the expression for angular momentum about the IC is:

\[\vec{K}_{IC} = I_{IC} \vec{\omega}\]

Finally, if we wish to find the angular momentum about any point P, there are two expressions. The subscripts for mass moment of inertia and linear velocity must match within the expression being used (i.e. both G or both P). Note that when we use linear momentum in the expression for angular momentum, the linear velocity is still always at the centre of gravity. Finally, if we wish to use the version with P subscripts, then P must be a point on the body (otherwise the linear velocity of P does not make sense).

\[\vec{K}_P = I_G \vec{\omega} + \vec{r}_{G/P} \times m \vec{v_G} = I_G \vec{\omega} + \vec{r}_{G/P} \times \vec{J}\]
OR
\[\vec{K}_P = I_P \vec{\omega} + \vec{r}_{G/P} \times m \vec{v_P} \]
