Free Rigid Body Collisions

A free rigid body collision is an instance where two non-anchored or moveable bodies impact one another, and we are treating at least one of those bodies as a rigid body where rotation can occur. In these instances, the large internal impulsive forces between the bodies colliding dwarf any external forces in the moment. The result is that though each body individually will have large changes in linear momentum in the instant of the collision, the linear momentum of both bodies as a whole is maintained.

\[ \sum\limits_{system} \vec{J}_f= \sum\limits_{system} \vec{J}_i \]
\[ \vec{J}_{Af} + \vec{J}_{Bf} = \vec{J}_{Ai} + \vec{J}_{Bi} \]
\[ m_{A} \vec{v_{GAf}}+m_{B} \vec{v_{GBf}}= m_{A} \vec{v_{GAi}}+m_{B} \vec{v_{GBi}}\]

Where A and B are two objects colliding. Recall that linear momentum uses the velocity of the centre of gravity of an object, and we cannot use any othe velocity.

Angular momentum is typically also conserved for the system, as the large impact impulse is internal.

\[ \sum\limits_{system} \vec{K_P}_f= \sum\limits_{system} \vec{K_P}_i \]
\[ \vec{K}_{P,Af} + \vec{K}_{P,Bf} = \vec{K}_{P,Ai} + \vec{K}_{P,Bi} \]
\[ I_{P,A} \vec{\omega_{Af}} + \vec{r}_{G_A/P} \times m_A \vec{v_{P,Af}} + I_{P,B} \vec{\omega_{Bf}} + \vec{r}_{G_B/P} \times m_B \vec{v_{P,Bf}} \cdots \]
\[ \,\,\, = I_{P,A} \vec{\omega_{Ai}} + \vec{r}_{G_A/P} \times m_A \vec{v_{P,Ai}} + I_{P,B} \vec{\omega_{Bi}} + \vec{r}_{G_B/P} \times m_B \vec{v_{P,Bi}} \]
OR
\[ I_{G,A} \vec{\omega_{Af}} + \vec{r}_{G_A/P} \times m_A \vec{v_{G,Af}} + I_{G,B} \vec{\omega_{Bf}} + \vec{r}_{G_B/P} \times m_B \vec{v_{G,Bf}} \cdots \]
\[ \,\,\, = I_{G,A} \vec{\omega_{Ai}} + \vec{r}_{G_A/P} \times m_A \vec{v_{G,Ai}} + I_{G,B} \vec{\omega_{Bi}} + \vec{r}_{G_B/P} \times m_B \vec{v_{G,Bi}} \]

Note that the angular momenta need to be taken about the same point for both bodies in order to be able to add them together.

In addition, angular momentum will also be conserved for each body individually about the point of impact. This is because the impact forces by definition cannot exert a moment about this point (and typically other impulse moments will be so much smaller they can be considered negligible). With no moment, there can be no change in angular momentum. Moments are force times distance and we are ensuring the distance is zero by choosing the impact point where the forces are exerted.

The angular momentum of one of the bodies (similar for the other), where Q is the impact point, will be:

\[ I_{G,A} \vec{\omega_{Af}} + \vec{r}_{G_A/Q} \times m_A \vec{v_{G,Af}} = I_{G,A} \vec{\omega_{Ai}} + \vec{r}_{G_A/Q} \times m_A \vec{v_{G,Ai}}\]
OR
\[ I_{Q,A} \vec{\omega_{Af}} + \vec{r}_{G_A/Q} \times m_A \vec{v_{Q,Af}} = I_{Q,A} \vec{\omega_{Ai}} + \vec{r}_{G_A/Q} \times m_A \vec{v_{Q,Ai}}\]

You can sometimes find conservation of linear momentum in the transverse direction, or perpendicular to the line of impact. This may not be true when there is friction at the impact point, because the friction force will be proportional to the impact (normal) force.

Finally, we can also use the coefficient of restitution equation, if we know something about the elastic/plastic characteristics of the collision.

\[e=\frac{\Delta v_{separation} }{\Delta v_{closing}}\]
\[e=\frac{v_{A,Qnf} - v_{B,Qnf}}{v_{B,Qni} - v_{A,Qni}}\]

Where Q is the point of impact, n is the direction of the line of impact (LOI), and A and B are the two bodies impacting.

For two dimensional problems, we can break the linear impulse equation down into two scalar components to solve. In this case, we simply need to break all forces and velocities into x and y components, taking these velocities at the center of mass of each body. Since all rotation will be about the z-axis for planar problems, the angular impulse momentum equation remains a single equation.

Along with kinematic relationships, combinations of the above equations and strategies can allow us to analyze post-impact motion of the objects. This is useful in areas such as accident reconstruction, where engineers determine happened in a vehicular crash.
