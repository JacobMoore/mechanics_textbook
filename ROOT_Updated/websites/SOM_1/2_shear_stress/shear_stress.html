Shear Stress

Shear stress is the counterpart to normal stress. Rather than being stresses in a plane perpendicular to the load forces. Shear stress exists between a set of non-collinear forces in a cross section that is parallel to the forces themselves. Using a pair of scissors to cut paper is a classic example of shear stress in action.

Similar to the definition of normal stress, the magnitude of shear stress is simply defined as the magnitude of the force divided by the cross-sectional area we are examining. The important thing to remember here is that the area will be parallel with the forces, rather than perpendicular to the surfaces. A lower case tau is traditionally used to denote shear stress.

\[\tau=V/A\]

Just like normal stress, the metric unit for shear stress is a Pascal (Pa), Kilopascal (kPa), or Megapascal (MPa). Similarly, units of pound per square inch (psi), or kilo-pounds per square inch (ksi) are the standard in the US system.

Non-Planar Shear

Although the cross sectional area in our shear stress calculations is typically in a single plane it is not always in a single plane. An example of non-planar shear stress is a paper hole punch. Similar to the pair of scissors discussed above, it is using shear stress to cut the paper. Unlike the scissors, the resulting cut surface is not a single geometric plane.

When determining the shear stress present in these cases, we will still use the same force divided by area formula. The only difference is that the area is not a single plane, it is the area along which the cut or fracture would occur. In our hole punch example, we would use the circumference times the height of the hole we punched (the area of the sides of our new cylindrical hole). Whatever area would be newly exposed by the punch or other tool, that is the area the stress is acting in and the area we use in our formulas.
